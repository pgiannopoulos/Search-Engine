Matthew Fisher: ‘We will have a real Christmas,’ says nun in Iraqi town nearly destroyed by ISIL

Facebook | Twitter | Google+ | Email

Matthew Fisher
Friday, Dec. 23, 2016

Bartella, Iraq — From the ruins of a home in this deserted, once overwhelmingly Christian town, a trio of scavengers had plucked what was a sorry-looking Christmas tree and were spiriting it away in a pickup truck.

The tree was the sole hint of Christmas cheer on the eastern outskirts of Mosul. With savage fighting between Iraqi troops and ISIL continuing in Iraq’s second-largest city, this venerable cradle of Christianity on the Nineveh plains will be almost bereft of worshippers at Christmas Mass for the third consecutive winter after what had been an uninterrupted but increasingly problematic run of nearly 2,000 years.

Signs of ISIL’s attempts to destroy Christianity in Iraq are to be found everywhere in these recently liberated towns. The courtyard of a damaged Syriac Catholic Church in the Christian town of Khidr Elias that ISIL used as its regional headquarters was filled with graffiti glorifying jihad. Painted over, but still legible, were words in praise of ISIL, quotations from the Qur’an and an elegant riff comparing Islam to a palm tree in the desert.

Christian militiamen who now squatted triumphantly on the church grounds had counter-attacked with graffiti of their own mocking ISIL’s propaganda about ruling for eternity. The new messaging was clear: Christianity was back to stay.

Despite the recent defeat of ISIL from some of the Christian areas in northwestern Iraq, “we will not have a light Christmas,” said Sister Diana, a member of the Syriac Catholic Church.

The nun said she had been meditating about the nature of the Christmas celebration. “Despite what we have suffered, we will have a real Christmas of understanding of the birth of Jesus on a cold night at a difficult time. Because that, too, is our situation,” she said.

Christianity in the Middle East has been ripped apart by ISIL but it has faced persecution since even before the Crusades. Tamerlane, or Timur, was said to have had 150,000 Christians beheaded in Baghdad and Tikrit in the 14th century.

Matthew Fisher / Postmedia NewsA crucifix survived the damage done to St. George Syriac Catholic Church in the Christian town of Bartella in Iraq. Islamic State in Iraq and the Levant singled churches out for destruction during the 27 months they occupied much of Iraq's Ninenveh Plain.

In 1947, as much as 12 per cent of the population of Iraq was Christian. The pre-2003 Christian population may have been as high as 1.5 million, or five per cent. It is believed to have fallen as low as two per cent today.

Christian groups have claimed that as many as 243 churches had been destroyed by ISIL during the latest convulsions, although it is hard to see how anyone could come up with an accurate account in what is a vast moonscape of booby-trapped roads and buildings in towns such as Bartella and Khidr Elias.

St. George’s in Bartella is one of the few churches in this area that is more or less intact, though a statue of the Virgin Mary had been battered, the graveyard attacked and a crypt there still contains a live rocket round with a jerry-rigged propane tank bomb near the entrance. Much of the church’s interior, including the altar, has been gutted, but most of the pews were untouched.

Near the nave of the church, shards of glass surround a large cross that was recovered from the wreckage, as was a purple velvet pennant with a cross stitched on it.

Whatever the number of churches that were blown up or burned down, there is no question that ISIL attacked Christianity in northwestern Iraq with the goal of exterminating it. When the jihadists seized Mosul in the summer of 2014, Assyrian Christians were told they must pay a tribute of nearly $500 per family, convert to Islam or be executed. This was later relaxed. In return for everything they owned, Christians could flee for their lives. 

Even the graves of our saints were destroyed. Nothing was sacred to (ISL)

In a mass exodus, more than 100,000 Christians who survived ISIL’s onslaught left Mosul for Baghdad, Turkey or the Erbil suburb of Ankawa, where churches are bound to be packed on Christmas Eve.

In Khidr Elias, three heavily armed young men from the Christian wing of the Ninevah Protection Unit talked about their hopes for the coming holiday as they stood watch over a distribution of relief packages by Samaritan’s Purse, a Christian humanitarian agency.

Matuos (Matthew), spoke nostalgically of the peaceful Christmases of his childhood, saying he was greatly looking forward to publicly celebrating the birth of Christ again without ISIL threatening to murder him for doing so.

“They tried to kill all of us and our traditional practices,” he said, his body wrapped in a bandolier of bullets, with an AK-47 assault rifle and a black mask hiding his face.

“Even the graves of our saints were destroyed. Nothing was sacred to them.”

Beside him, Are, which means hero, proffered his heavily tattooed right arm, which sported a large Catholic cross. Such markings would have been a death sentence if anyone from ISIL had demanded that he roll up his sleeve, he said.

Matuos acknowledged that with lots of bombs still going off and an ISIL ambush possible at any moment, it was still too dangerous for those in his family who had survived to return to their homes here.

The U.S., the EU and Britain have claimed that what has happened to Christian communities across the Middle East and especially in Iraq and Syria over the past few years is genocide. To cries of “shame” from the opposition, Prime Minister Justin Trudeau’s government voted down a Conservative motion on this last June by a vote of 166 to 139. The prime minister argued that “determinations of a genocide need to be made in an objective, responsible way.” This was a question for the UN to consider, he said.

“What the Canadian government did is ignorance and it makes me really mad,” said Sister Diana. “Those who make such decisions have lost nothing. They sit in their nice homes and make such decisions.

“Our people live in tents and suffer in the cold. Look at what ISIL did. This whole area is a crime scene. They captured women and tortured and raped them. They murdered us. They wrecked our churches.”

Matthew Fisher travelled to Kurdish Iraq with Samaritan’s Purse of Canada, a humanitarian aid agency.
