Jaromir Jagr, from party animal to — at age 44 — NHL second-highest scorer after passing Mark Messier

Facebook | Twitter | Google+ | Email

Colby Cosh
Friday, Dec. 23, 2016

Jaromir Jagr has wrested the all-time scoring lead among mortal NHL players from his old teammate Mark Messier. AP File Photo

Let us all hail Jaromir Jagr, who earned an assist Thursday night with his latest team, the Florida Panthers, and became the NHL’s second-highest career scorer of all time. It was not the most glorious of Jagr’s 1,888 National Hockey League points. Some reports described the assist as involving the puck bouncing off his rear end, but even this was an honourable exaggeration. The disc barely touched his CCMs before Finn phenom Aleksander Barkov tapped it home past the Bruins’ Tuukka Rask.

They all count. It is extraordinary enough that Jagr is playing hockey in any professional setting at the age of 44, let alone screening an NHL goalie for someone less than half his age.

Jagr has wrested the all-time scoring lead amongst the mortal NHL players from his old teammate Mark Messier, the great local idol of northern Alberta hockey. I cannot be expected to approve wholly of this, but there is a certain poetic balance to it. Many sportswriters are observing that Jagr did not seem like a strong bet to become one of history’s most durable NHLers. Much the same could be said of Messier.

Messier’s origins were quite different from Jagr’s: for years he seemed slightly confused about which end was up in the pro game. He did not even switch to playing centre until he was already an All-Star. Jagr, by contrast, came into the league as an explosive teenaged heavy-metal genius. But both men carried reputations as tireless party animals well into their thirties, even as they seemed to change species before our eyes. Messier evolved into something resembling the boss of a prison gang in a comic book. Jagr’s visage, once that of a flirtatious, cheeky manchild, is now gray-bearded and sober — just grim enough to remind us that his surname is a Bohemian variation of the German “jäger.” After all these years, he is finally starting to look the part of a proper hunter.

When Soviet-bloc players first started to cross over to the NHL, some of them saw their careers crash quickly when they were suddenly confronted with North American money, dietary choices, and personal freedom. It might have been easy to mistake Jagr for a “Soviet” star and expect dissipation and rapid aging, although he hated communism (his number, 68, commemorates the Prague Spring). He came to North America not as an exile or a nostalgia act: it was an environment he had dreamed of, had craved, and it allowed him to thrive.

His physical conditioning was said to be somewhat casual when he was with the Pittsburgh Penguins, but it has intensified along with his age. He could probably no longer win a straight-line speed contest with a basset hound. But he is still given plenty of space by defenders. No one has yet thought of a rule-compliant way to take Jagr’s hands away from Jagr.

A major theme of his career in eight NHL cities has been his love of being on the ice at all hours. Maybe it is not so important to spend hours on Nautilus machines if you are skating when all your teammates are asleep. Maybe the joie de vivre he shares with the conquered Messier — a devious, brutal player who ended up earning millions in advertising money by virtue of an irresistible giggle — has something to do with their mutual success.

Jagr left the NHL for a few years to play in Russia, to be closer to his Czech home. But he seems to have found that the best hockey league in the world was his rightful place in an equally compelling sense. He has hunted down everyone who ever played in the league but Gretzky, put them all behind him. Gretzky remains 969 points, a whole Hall of Fame career ahead, in the official table.

AP Photo/Wilfredo LeeA grizzled Jaromir Jagr speaks to the media on Sept. 22, 2016, as the Florida Panthers reported to the 2016 training camp.

Some analysts who are fond of comparing players by means of “era adjustments” will tell you that in context, Jagr has been almost as voluminous a scorer as 99. Happy as I am to eulogize Jagr, my admiration stops short of this. Gretzky did play in a higher-scoring league against goalies who do not meet today’s standards: they guarded nets of the same size with much smaller equipment, and were far too dignity-conscious to perform gymnastics merely to stop a puck.

Some adjustment for this is appropriate. But most of the statisticians trying to shrink Gretzky’s numbers to comprehensibility — and that is largely the point of the exercise — make additional deductions that are speculative at best, unfair at worst. Either way, Messier’s big shiny head will be the last one mounted on Jagr’s wall. No one need be ashamed of such a trophy.
